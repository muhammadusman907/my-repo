1971 Original Rule: OSHA was established, and the requirement for employers to maintain records of work-related injuries and illnesses was introduced. The focus was on basic recordkeeping to track workplace injuries and illnesses. The first standardized log known as Form 200 was established.

1982 Partial Exemption for Low-Risk Industries: OSHA introduced a partial exemption for specific low-risk industries, exempting certain establishments from the requirement to maintain injury and illness records. This exemption was designed for industries with consistently low injury and illness rates, as determined by the Standard Industrial Classification (SIC) system or the North American Industry Classification System (NAICS). It aimed to reduce the administrative burden on these industries while still ensuring the safety of workers.

1996 Amendments: OSHA revised the recordkeeping requirements to simplify the forms and reduce the burden on employers while still capturing essential information about workplace injuries and illnesses.

2001 Update: OSHA introduced the OSHA 300 Log, 301 Incident Report, and 300A Summary form. The 300 Log provides a detailed record of each incident, the 301 Report captures additional information about the incident, and the 300A Summary provides a high-level overview of the recorded incidents for each year.

2015 Electronic Reporting Rule: OSHA announced updates to require certain employers to electronically submit their injury and illness data. This move aimed to improve access to workplace safety information for workers, employers, researchers, and the public.

2016 Anti-Retaliation Rule: OSHA introduced provisions to improve employee rights and prevent retaliation against workers who report workplace injuries or illnesses. This rule emphasized the importance of creating a culture of safety where employees feel comfortable reporting incidents without fear of reprisal.

2019 Electronic Reporting Requirements Expanded: OSHA expanded the electronic reporting requirements, requiring establishments with 250 or more employees to electronically submit information from their OSHA 300, 300A, and 301 forms annually. Establishments with 20-249 employees in certain high-risk industries were also required to submit summary data.

2020 Final Rule on Electronic Data Submission: OSHA revised the electronic data submission requirements, rescinding the requirement for establishments with 250 or more employees to electronically submit information from the OSHA 300 and 301 forms. However, these establishments are still required to submit information from their OSHA 300A Summary form annually.
